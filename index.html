How to Stay a Child

Poem
At six, my throat was a bird I didn't know I owned. Every sound I made felt stolen from somewhere holy. I didn't yet know the words diaphragm or placement, didn't know I was supposed to be building something.
Somewhere between then and now, between the first song and the five hundredth, I learned a language I wish I hadn't: the jokes we make to prove we're not naive. The way we talk about the work now— like it's something to survive, to get through, like wonder is for amateurs, like caring too much is embarrassing.
But hey: my voice is a physics problem that solved itself without asking permission. Meat and air and the vibration of small bones making something from nothing, every single time.
The child who sang in the car, who didn't know cool from uncool, is still here, underneath the armor of knowing better than to be impressed.
First class on an airplane—I took pictures of the napkins. By the third flight, I was asleep before takeoff. That first bite of something perfect, and by the end I'm scrolling through my phone. We kill everything by having it twice. We kill it by getting good at it.
So now I practice wonder like I practice runs. I make a fool of myself with thank you. Before I walk onstage, I touch my own throat like I'm meeting it for the first time. I'm training myself in a different discipline: the art of forgetting I know how to do this. The practice of meeting my own voice like it's a stranger's gift I get to borrow. Because it is. Because it always was.
This body that makes sound. This feeling that came. This breath that keeps arriving.
Mastery wants me tired and superior. Experience wants me to roll my eyes. But I'm learning to stay dumb enough to be shocked that any of this is happening— to sing each song like it's the first song, to stay so easily amazed that my whole life remains what it was when I was six: impossible, and here.

At the Threshold

Essay

This morning, I picked a cactus fruit.

The desert had offered it to me the way the desert offers everything—without ceremony, without expectation. Just: here. The skin was thick as secrets, studded with fine spines that required patience to navigate. Inside, the flesh was the color of pomegranates, sweet enough to make me stop walking, close my eyes, let the taste root me to that particular square of earth under that particular sky.

I was alone. I was also held by everything.

This is what I know about loneliness now: it is not the opposite of love. It is love's strange twin, the one that arrives when you've learned to see clearly in the dark.

I spend my life at thresholds.

Concert halls where I stand before hundreds of faces and sing Caroline Shaw's "To the Hands," that prayer for the displaced, and feel the particular ache of knowing that most people in those seats will hear the notes but miss the ceremony underneath—the small altar I'm building with my breath, the way I'm trying to say in music what cannot be said in words: we are all so far from home, and isn't that the point, and can't we love each other anyway?

Hospital rooms where I hold the hand of my eighty-six-year-old friend Carol Ann, her fingers thin as bird bones in mine, both of us crying and laughing because she's alive, she's still here, and we've loved each other across fifteen years and the Atlantic Ocean and every impossible thing that makes love both essential and insufficient.

Empty houses in cities that aren't mine, waiting for WeChat messages from Guangzhou about my mother's next move into a retirement home I researched but will never see, coordinating her care in Cantonese across thirteen time zones while eating cheap sushi alone, my body so tired it forgets how to be anything but functional.

I am often the smartest person in the room. This is not a boast. It is a fact that weighs exactly as much as the cactus fruit in my palm—real, heavy, harder to hold than you'd think.

I am almost always the loneliest.

For years, I believed this meant I was doing something wrong. That if I could just be kinder, clearer, more patient—if I could translate myself into language people could receive without flinching—someone would finally meet me where I stand.

Then Mother Ayahuasca spoke to me, and everything became both simpler and more complex.

She said: You are my deputy.

Not chosen for worthiness. Not blessed. Just: assigned. The way some people are born with perfect pitch or double-jointed thumbs. A structural fact of how I move through the world.

Deputy must be tough, she said. Deputy must work alone.

I wanted to argue. But then, more gently, she added: Loneliness is the work. Not your life.

And something in me—some small, clenched fist I didn't know I was making—opened.

The work is this: standing at the threshold between sleeping and waking. Seeing clearly and staying present with all of it. Loving people who cannot love you back at the same depth. Being available when someone finally stumbles toward consciousness, even knowing most won't. Carrying medicine in the form of poems, songs, presence, clarity—and offering it freely, without attachment to whether anyone receives it.

But here is what Ayahuasca taught me to distinguish: The work is the loneliness of the threshold. My life is the cactus fruit.

My life is not the loneliness. The loneliness is what I carry while living fully, the way the moon carries darkness while radiating light.

觀音—Guanyin, the Bodhisattva of Compassion—understands this. She stands at thresholds too. She has a thousand arms because two will never be enough for the amount of suffering she witnesses. And she stays.

聞聲救苦—hearing sounds, relieving suffering. This is the vow. Not to save everyone. Not to make everyone understand. Just to be present. To offer what can be offered. To love without requiring love in return.

Stop apologizing for being extraordinary. You are not too much for this world. You are exactly the size that love made you.

The world is smaller than you. That is not your fault.

善意是餘光. Kindness is the residual light. Love is the residual light. Every real connection leaves a trace, changes the shape of who you are, adds to the medicine you carry. Nothing is wasted. Not even the pain.

Tomorrow morning, I will wake in my own bed, finally home. I will look out the window and find something beautiful. I will speak a quiet prayer. I will remember: loneliness is the work, not my life. And then I will go stand at the threshold again.

And if you're standing at a threshold too — 我哋一齊. We are together.

日月明. Sun and moon. Day and night. Solitude and company. Light and understanding. All of it necessary. All of it holy.

We are deputy. We are 觀音's hands. And we are doing this right.
